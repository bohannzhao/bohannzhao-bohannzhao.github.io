Bohan Zhao

Welcome! I am an AHA postdoctoral fellow in the Neurometabolism Lab led by Prof. Li Ye at Scripps Research/HHMI. My current research focuses on brain-body communication, specifically investigating how adipose tissue interacts with the central nervous system to regulate behavior and physiology. Using advanced techniques such as viral-genetic tracing, whole-brain clearing, and in vivo large-scale imaging, I aim to uncover how disruptions in this communication contribute to metabolic and emotional disorders.

Prior to joining Scripps in 2023, I earned my Ph.D. in neuroscience under the mentorship of Prof. Yi Zhong at Tsinghua University. My doctoral work uncovered critical mechanisms of memory formation in Drosophila, including the discovery of "context-dependent memory" and "merged memory". After completing my Ph.D., I conducted a brief postdoctoral research with Prof. Gero Miesenböck at the University of Oxford, where I explored neural circuit mechanisms underlying sleep regulation, gaining expertise in in vivo electrophysiology and multiphoton imaging.

Research

My current research interests lie in how sensory information affects behavior and cognition, with a focus on developing therapeutic strategies for neurological and psychological disorders.
Long-term memory is formed immediately without the need for protein synthesis-dependent consolidation in Drosophila
Zhao B, Sun J, Zhang X, Mo H, Niu Y, Li Q, Wang L, Zhong Y.
Nat Commun 10, 4550 (2019). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-019-12436-7

In our study, we discovered a novel context-dependent long-term memory (cLTM) that lasts a lifetime without decay. Surprisingly, this memory forms within just five minutes and does not require new protein synthesis, challenging the traditional understanding of the molecular basis of long-term memory. Unlike canonical memories stored in the mushroom body, cLTM is encoded in the lateral horn, a higher-order olfactory center. Retrieval of cLTM relies on multisensory integration, with sensory systems converging in the lateral horn to elicit olfactory avoidance. This work redefines our understanding of memory storage, highlighting the lateral horn’s role in lifelong memory and sensory processing.

Differential conditioning produces merged long-term memory in Drosophila
Zhao B, Sun J, Li Q, Zhong Y
eLife 10:e66499 (2021). https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.66499

We explored the distinction between remembering an event and discriminating its details. We discovered a unique phenomenon called “merged memory,” where a blurry memory associates negative valence with the entire event rather than specific details. This finding reveals how memories can generalize emotional experiences, prioritizing the overall emotional context over precise details. It provides new insights into how the brain processes and stores emotionally charged events, with implications for understanding memory distortions and emotional decision-making.

Exclusion and Co-expression of Aversive Olfactory Long-Term Memories in Drosophila
Zhao B, Zhang X, Zhao J, Li Q
Neurosci Bull 38, 657-660 (2022). https://doi.org/10.1007/s12264-022-00830-z

Building on above findings, we investigated how different types of memory interact to guide behavior. Our work supports a model describing the interplay between spaced long-term memory (spLTM), context-dependent LTM (cLTM), and merged LTM (mLTM).

Genetic dissection of mutual interference between two consecutive learning tasks in Drosophila
Zhao J, Zhang X, Zhao B, Hu W, Diao T, Wang L, Li Q
eLife 12:e83516 (2023). https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.83516

We investigated the molecular mechanisms of mutual interference between two successive learning tasks. Our finding supports that the proactive interference involves the CSW/Raf/MAPK pathway, whereas retroactive interference is more sensitive to the Rac1-mediated active forgetting pathway.
